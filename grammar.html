1. Introduction to Swahili (Kiswahili)

Swahili, known natively as Kiswahili, is a Bantu language spoken by over 100 million people across East Africa. It's the official language of Tanzania, Kenya, and Uganda, and serves as a lingua franca throughout the region.

Key characteristics of Swahili:

  • Uses the Latin alphabet (no special characters needed)
  • Highly phonetic - words are pronounced as written
  • Agglutinative language - prefixes and suffixes modify meaning
  • Uses noun classes instead of gender
  • Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) word order

2. Alphabet & Pronunciation

Swahili uses 24 letters of the Latin alphabet. The letters Q and X are not used.

Vowels (Vokali)

Swahili has 5 pure vowels, each with one consistent sound:

3. Noun Classes (Ngeli)

Swahili has 18 noun classes, but learners typically focus on the most common ones. Noun classes affect prefixes on nouns, adjectives, and verbs.

Main Noun Classes

ClassSingularPluralExamples

M-MI (Trees/Plants)m-mi-mti/miti (tree/trees)
KI-VI (Things)ki-vi-kiti/viti (chair/chairs)
N-N (Various)n-n-nyumba/nyumba (house/houses)
JI-MA (Fruits/Large things)ji-/0ma-jicho/macho (eye/eyes)

Mtoto mdogo anacheza.

7. Sentence Structure

Basic Swahili follows Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order, similar to English:

Juma anasoma kitabu.

Juma is reading a book.

Subject (Juma) + Verb (anasoma) + Object (kitabu)

The Verb "To Be" (Kuwa)

Present tense "to be" is often expressed with ni (am/is/are):

Mimi ni mwanafunzi. / Yeye ni daktari.

I am a student. / He/She is a doctor.

9. Forming Questions (Maswali)

Question Words

SwahiliEnglishExample
Nani?Who?Nani anakuja? (Who is coming?)
Nini?What?Unafanya nini? (What are you doing?)
Wapi?Where?Unakwenda wapi? (Where are you going?)
Lini?When?Utakuja lini? (When will you come?)
Kwa nini?Why?Kwa nini unalia? (Why are you crying?)
Vipi? / Namna gani?How?Unajisikiaje? (How do you feel?)
Ngapi?How many?Watoto wangapi? (How many children?)

Yes/No Questions

Yes/No questions are formed by raising intonation at the end, or by adding "Je" at the beginning:

Je, unasema Kiswahili? / Unakuja?

Do you speak Swahili? / Are you coming?

10. Negation (Ukanushaji)

Negation in Swahili changes based on tense and subject:

Present Tense Negation

Use ha- prefix + subject marker + verb (no -na-):

Sisomi. / Hasomi. / Hatusomi.

I don't read. / He/She doesn't read. / We don't read.

Negative Subject Prefixes

PositiveNegativeExample
ni- (I)si-Sisemi (I don't speak)
u- (you)hu-Husemi (You don't speak)
a- (he/she)ha-Hasemi (He/She doesn't speak)
tu- (we)hatu-Hatusemi (We don't speak)
m- (you pl)ham-Hamsemi (You all don't speak)
wa- (they)hawa-Hawasemi (They don't speak)

Past & Future Negation

TenseMarkerExample
Past Negative-ku-Sikukuja (I didn't come)
Future Negative-ta- + ending -iSitakuja (I won't come)
Perfect Negative-ja-Sijala (I haven't eaten)
